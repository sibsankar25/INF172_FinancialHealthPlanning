Problem

HSAs can be confusing—especially for students navigating insurance terms, tradeoffs, and first-time benefits decisions.

Why this matters

Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) are often presented alongside plan selection and employer benefits, but many undergraduates have limited exposure to healthcare financing and related terminology. This can reduce confidence and lead to missed opportunities to learn how HSAs work in practice.

Preliminary survey of 26 undergraduate students.

Design goal

Explore whether a lightweight, scenario-based learning game can improve familiarity with HSA concepts through repeated decision-making and feedback.

Research & Findings

Preliminary results from an early-stage undergraduate survey exploring HSA awareness, adoption, and interest in a game-based learning approach.

61.2%
reported they don’t know what an HSA is
96.2%
reported they do not have an HSA
88.5%
reported they would be willing to play the game after seeing proposed designs
80.8%
reported they don’t have a solid understanding of HSAs

Method

  • Google Forms survey
  • Convenience sample of undergraduate students
  • n = 26

Limitations: Early-stage, preliminary findings; results may not generalize.

Interpretation (optional to add later): self-rated understanding skewed low (1–2) and interest in a game format was high, supporting an educational intervention worth prototyping and testing.

Key takeaway

Results suggest low baseline familiarity and low adoption, with high willingness to engage with an interactive learning format.

  • Knowledge gap: many students do not recognize or understand HSAs
  • Adoption gap: most students do not have an HSA
  • Engagement signal: students report willingness to try the game format

The Game

A scenario-based learning game that uses decisions and feedback to teach HSA concepts in context.

How it works

Players make scenario-based decisions involving budgeting, simulated healthcare events, and plan tradeoffs. The game provides visual feedback on HSA balance and out-of-pocket costs to show the consequences of choices over time.

  • Scenario-based decisions
  • Budgeting and tradeoffs
  • Simulated healthcare events
  • Visual feedback on HSA balance and out-of-pocket costs

How it helps

  • Build familiarity with terms
  • Increase confidence selecting plans
  • Learn by doing via consequences/feedback
